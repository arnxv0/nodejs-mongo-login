What is a sleep cycle?

A sleep cycle is a recurring pattern of sleep stages that occurs during a night's sleep. The sleep cycle is typically composed of four distinct stages: NREM1 (non-rapid eye movement stage 1), NREM2, NREM3 (also known as slow-wave sleep or deep sleep), and REM (rapid eye movement) sleep. These stages repeat cyclically throughout the night, with each cycle lasting approximately 90 to 110 minutes.
1.NREM1 (Light Sleep): This is the transition from wakefulness to sleep. It lasts only a few minutes, during which you may experience muscle relaxation and the beginning of the sleep process.

2.NREM2 (Light Sleep): This stage is characterized by a slightly deeper sleep, and it accounts for a significant portion of total sleep time. During NREM2, your heart rate and breathing become more regular, and your body temperature decreases.

3.NREM3 (Deep Sleep): This is the stage of deep or slow-wave sleep. It is important for physical restoration, growth, and immune function. During this stage, the body repairs muscles and tissues, stimulates growth and development, boosts immune function, and builds up energy for the next day.

4.REM (Dream Sleep): REM sleep is characterized by rapid eye movements, increased brain activity, and vivid dreams. It is thought to be crucial for cognitive function, memory consolidation, and emotional regulation. The first REM period typically occurs about 90 minutes after falling asleep and lasts for about 10 minutes, with each subsequent REM period becoming longer.

Throughout the night, a person goes through multiple sleep cycles, with the proportion of time spent in each stage varying. It's important to complete several cycles to achieve restorative and restful sleep. Waking up during a specific stage, especially deep sleep, can lead to feelings of grogginess and sleep inertia. The entire sleep cycle is orchestrated by the brain and is essential for overall health and well-being.
